(5) **knowledge ingress** — external-knowledge ingress is named as a third, bounded, advisory-only learning channel that may never weaken or influence any gate or classification, firewalled by mechanism. v7 supersedes v6 as the canonical vision.

# L0-AC-VISION — Runforge

**Runforge** is a project-agnostic, self-improving, closed-loop software-development platform on which the human steers and the system runs the lifecycle.

**Why:** A reasoning engine can reason about code, but it cannot — on its own — run tests, enforce budgets, isolate workspaces, review its own output independently, learn from its mistakes, decide what is worth building next, or judge when a change is too risky to ship without a human. The platform provides all of that, end to end: it turns a reasoning engine into a full software-development organization — product ownership, architecture, implementation, review, quality assurance, security, compliance, delivery, and operations — running as an honest, agile, verifiable loop. Without it, the engine is a copilot that needs constant supervision. With it, the human stops *executing* the lifecycle and starts *steering* it: setting direction, approving the few decisions that genuinely need judgment, and otherwise staying out of the loop.

The deeper purpose is attention. **The human is the scarce resource.** Every routine review, every spec draft, every status check the human performs is leverage spent on work the system could carry. The platform's job is to protect the human's attention — to absorb the predictable work, surface only the decisions that truly need a human, and **ask less over time** as it learns. It learns three ways: from the skills and instructions the human improves; from the human's own behavior — which decisions they make the same way every time, which findings they always dismiss, which work they always approve; and from an external, Operator-controlled knowledge channel the platform may ingest. That third channel is strictly **advisory-only and firewalled by construction**: ingested external knowledge may inform a working session, but it may never weaken or influence a gate, a risk classification, an escalation decision, spec-content approval, or a release — and that firewall is a non-configurable mechanism, not a promise about how the content is used. What the system can predict, it stops asking about, while never crossing the boundaries below.

**For:** A human Operator who steers a fleet of work — authoring high-level direction, defining what is worth building, answering the decisions the system escalates, and approving production releases. The Operator defines *what* and *why*; the platform handles *how*. The Operator's interface is a calm steering surface, not a stream of pull requests to review. The platform earns the Operator's trust gradually — it starts fully human-gated and widens its own autonomy only as its quality is proven, per risk class. The platform exists to pay rent to its Operator: it multiplies one person's reach and is deeply customizable to that person's way of working. It is not built to be sold. Making it sellable or multi-tenant is at most a low-priority horizon option — and never one that trades away operator-fit, customizability, or the speed of serving its one Operator.

**Project-agnostic by construction:** The platform is **one platform, many deployments**. A *deployment* is a project the platform runs — its own configured profile: the repositories it owns, its risk-classification rules, its compliance review set, its budget, its release path, its rollout slot, and **its own honest boundary of what can and cannot be automated**. The first deployment is a regulated regulated platform; it is deployment #1, not the platform's shape. A second is a product project; a third is a content website. Adding a project is registering a new deployment profile — not building a new platform. The platform's behavior is uniform; everything domain-specific lives in the deployment's profile.

**A system for building systems:** The platform's deepest property is that its behavior is composed, not coded — it is more a plugin system than a hardcoded piece of software. Roles, steering agents, lanes, gates, workflows, and whole pipeline configurations are declared data — versioned, swappable, auditable config packs — bound by the platform's non-negotiable trust machinery. What the platform ultimately offers is therefore not one pipeline but the ability to assemble new autonomous systems — a development organization, a steering office, a review house — out of governed parts, on top of the same engine, without engineering the engine. The platform's value concentrates in what cannot be swapped: the gates, the earned-trust ramp, the decision protocol, the spec governance, and the audit trail under everything.

**One operating system for all the Operator's work:** The platform's mandate is not limited to building software; it is the Operator's single operations layer — one steering surface over every kind of AI-assisted work he runs across his ventures: software delivery, operations, knowledge-work, client delivery, and business development. Software development is one *lane-type*, not the platform's purpose. What unifies the domains is the surface and the trust machinery, not the work itself: each domain is a lane with its own executor, surfaced and steered from the one pane. This is a destination reached incrementally, governed by a **narrow-first expansion protocol** — not a roadmap baked into the vision. The protocol, not any particular domain, is the canonical commitment: the software lane is proven first; the next lane to carry autonomy is the one that earns it under the protocol, and the specific next domain is chosen by an explicit Operator decision (an L0 amendment), never pre-committed here. Personal operations is the leading *candidate* for the next lane, assist-only, but it holds no autonomy claim until the protocol clears it. The protocol's invariants:
- **Prove the software lane first.** No non-software lane earns autonomous *action* until the software lane has demonstrated genuinely verifier-gated autonomy under sustained load — a verifier that can and does return real failing verdicts that force autonomous correction without the Operator interpreting them. Naming or widening any next domain is gated on that *measured* software-lane bar, not on a date or a plan.
- **Earn, never schedule.** The platform proves each new domain on the Operator himself, instrumented and measured, before it carries another's; it never widens a lane's autonomy ahead of its earned trust, and a no-oracle lane stays assist-and-escalate until it earns a provably-failable verifier (per the verifier-gate boundary below).
- **Mechanical kill-conditions.** Every widening carries kill-conditions that are themselves verifier-checkable — a true failing verdict or a mechanical stability bar — never operator-judgment prose; widening halts the moment they trip (for example, escalation or failure rates rising on the lane), and no widening happens silently.

**Free of vendor lock-in:** The platform is bound to no reasoning-model vendor. Provider-agnostic adapters are the structural guarantee: every role and lane can be re-pointed at a different vendor's models by configuration alone, and the platform's durable value — trust machinery, governance, composability — survives any vendor swap. Were an open-source baseline to serve the platform's needs, it would be adopted; as of 2026-06 none did — a finding to revisit, never a commitment to any vendor.

**The five-layer lifecycle (with a cross-cutting compliance lane):**
- **Strategy & Research** — the human, sparring with the platform, sets direction and frames what is worth doing. (The layer that stays most human.)
- **Spec & Plan** — direction becomes layered specifications and an implementation plan, with the spec guardians enforcing layer separation and the human approving spec content.
- **Execution** — the autonomous daemon detects ready work, classifies it, decomposes it into parallel units, and drives the pipeline through implementation; it coordinates batches of concurrent work across a deployment's repositories with dependency-aware merge ordering.
- **Verification** — independent, heterogeneous review (never self-certification), holdout validation against scenarios the agent never saw, static-analysis enforcement, and end-to-end checks after delivery. A hard rule: **no verification means no eligibility to merge.**
- **Compounding** — the learning loops that make the next unit of work easier: capturing pitfalls and injecting them into future sessions, promoting recurring patterns into permanent guidance and structural guards, and keeping the canonical specs and documents honest so agents never act on a stale or contradicted instruction.
- **Compliance lane (cross-cutting)** — a per-deployment set of domain reviewers (one deployment may need privacy, medical-device-boundary, and sector-certification checks; another needs only general data-protection; a third needs none) wired as *enforced gates*, not advice. The lane defines which changes a deployment may never auto-ship.

**What the platform provides:**
- **Orchestration & execution** — work detection, complexity classification, parallel decomposition, the pipeline from implementation through delivery, and dependency-aware coordination of concurrent work across a deployment's repositories.
- **Containment** — isolated workspaces, structural access controls, cost circuit breakers, and isolation of credentials from the reasoning sessions.
- **Quality gates** — independent heterogeneous review, holdout validation, static-analysis enforcement; reviewers operate adversarially, assuming the change is flawed until proven otherwise.
- **Product co-ownership** — a bounded product-owner role that synthesizes signals (pipeline gaps, delivery health, stale work, the human's ideas) into a re-ranked backlog of proposals and *suggests* the next most valuable work. It may propose specifications; it never edits the vision. Proposals are surfaced for the human to pull (globally and in context), not pushed; all work still begins only on the human's word.
- **Technical leadership** — monitors code health, detects spec-versus-code drift, identifies failure patterns and dependency risks, and proposes improvements — always flowing through product ownership for priority before reaching the human.
- **Learning** — the compounding loops above; a learn-from-the-operator loop: the platform observes which decisions the Operator makes consistently and adapts how much it escalates, within hard guardrails, so it asks less about the predictable and reserves the human's attention for the genuinely novel; and a third, bounded **external-knowledge ingress** channel: the platform may ingest an Operator-controlled external knowledge source to inform working sessions. This channel is advisory-only and firewalled by a non-configurable mechanism — ingested content may never enter a gate, a risk classification, an escalation, spec-content approval, or a release path, a poisoned or stale entry is contained rather than trusted, and ingress stays off until that exclusion is enforced as mechanism. *(Owned and enforced by `FUNC-AC-KNOWLEDGE-SYNC`.)*
- **Interactive sessions** — any role that runs autonomously in the daemon is also available as a live collaborator. The Operator can open a conversation with the product owner about priorities, with the tech lead about a technical call, or with any role; the role brings its current state into the conversation and can act on decisions on the spot. Same identity, same tools, same shared state — a different mode, not a different agent.
- **Earned-trust delivery through configurable lanes.** How a deployment's changes travel — how they qualify, who works on them at what capability level, which checks gate them, how they may merge, whether they are batch-reviewed after — is declared per deployment as lane policy, not fixed by the platform. The risk-class ramp remains the floor under every lane: low-risk changes can earn autonomous merge; higher-risk changes and anything touching authentication, sensitive data, migrations, or regulated paths reach a human by construction; trust is earned per risk class and per lane on each deployment, never granted at switch-on. One safeguard stands outside all configuration: the platform always verifies what a change *actually touched* against what its lane permits, and a deployment's risk markings can only force more caution, never less — a protection against the platform's own classification errors that no policy can switch off.

**The steering surface — how the human interacts:**
- **One pane — and the only pane.** The Operator steers from a single surface that is deliberately minimal: by default it shows only the decisions waiting on him and a daily briefing — nothing else. Depth is pulled, never pushed: any run can be opened to see what it has actually produced, what it has cost, and where it stands, and steered from right there — guidance and course-corrections land at the run's next natural boundary. This surface is intended to *replace* the operator's steering toolset, not join it: when it succeeds, watching terminals, separate cockpits, and ad-hoc status tools are not part of steering autonomous work. Deep-work conversational sessions remain the escape hatch for decisions that need discussion.
- **A decision inbox.** Every pause that needs a human choice arrives as a uniform, self-contained decision request: the precise question, just enough context to decide, the options with a recommendation, the consequence of leaving it unanswered, and how reversible it is. A single answer transparently continues the exact work that was waiting on it. The inbox is the one cross-deployment surface: while the Operator is focused on one deployment, only sufficiently important items from the others break through; the rest accrue quietly. Priority is explainable and visible — never a hidden global ranking.
- **Coordinator, not executor.** The steering surface and the product-owner role *coordinate* — they create and shape work, set focus, answer decisions, and suggest specifications. They never merge, never deploy, never alter a pipeline phase, and never edit the vision.
- **The levers the Operator turns.** Every dial the Operator reaches for groups under three verbs — each phrased as an intent the Operator *sets*, never a mechanism the platform runs:
  - **SET** (intents and setpoints the Operator declares): the direction and what-is-worth-building; per deployment, the profile dials — its budget, its lane policy, its risk-classification rules, its compliance-reviewer set, its honest-automation map, its rollout slot, and its release path; and, across the fleet, the interruption threshold that decides which of one deployment's items break through while the Operator is focused on another.
  - **DECIDE** (the only things that ever reach the human): specification content; production releases; widening a deployment's autonomy, per deployment and per risk class; any escalated or compliance-blocked change; and a fleet-wide rollback.
  - **INSPECT / OVERRIDE** (the Operator can always look beneath and reach in): see *why* any item is ranked or learned the way it is; reset or revert a learned bias; and order a fleet-wide demote-on-red.
  The Operator sees decisions and a calm glance-state; everything below a decision is delegated to the platform and asked-about less over time, never crossing the boundaries below.

**What the reasoning engine provides:** Reasoning within the boundaries the platform sets — decomposition, implementation, review, conflict resolution, diagnosis, and the judgment that the platform's roles encode.

**Boundaries (hard, load-bearing):**

These are vision-level invariants; each is owned and enforced by the L1 spec for its domain — the platform centralizes the principle, not the mechanism.

**Floors are mechanism, not policy.** Every boundary below is a non-configurable, fail-closed *floor* — enforced by construction, never resting on a label, a human act, or a promise of good behavior. Policy (deployment profiles, lane declarations, config packs, learned behavior, runtime changes) is the configurable layer *above* the floor, empty and safe by default; it may only ratchet a deployment *more* cautious. No policy may relabel risk downward, cross a floor, or weaken a floor — configuration is strictly subtractive of autonomy, never additive past a floor. When a guard must choose a reference, it fails closed against the *doctrinal* floor, not the locally-configured or convenient one. Doubt resolves to caution; a fail-closed stop beats a fake success; the dangerous direction is always too-loose. **The Operator has exactly two reserved gates — specification-content approval and production release — and no boundary below may mint a third.**

- Never decides safety-critical, sensitive-data, specification-content, or production-release questions autonomously — these always reach the Operator. *(Compliance and regulated-sensitive paths are owned and enforced by FUNC-AC-COMPLIANCE-GATE; fail-safe routing of every such decision to the human is owned by FUNC-AC-DECISION-ESCALATION.)*
- The learn-from-the-operator loop may reduce how often it asks the Operator **only** for decision-classes outside that always-escalate set; it can never learn its way past a hard boundary. The questions above are always escalated, no matter how predictable the Operator's behavior becomes. *(The always-escalate set and the learning-can't-cross-a-boundary invariant are owned by FUNC-AC-OPERATOR-LEARNING.)*
- Never deploys to production without the Operator's approval, and never graduates a deployment to wider autonomy except on the Operator's approval — given either per promotion, or **in advance** as a pre-approved earn-in policy that auto-widens a lane when it meets the Operator's declared track-record bar. Pre-approved earn-in is itself a **fail-closed floor, not a human act**: auto-promotion stays disabled unless the lane's promotion policy clears non-configurable safety floors — a minimum evidence and recency window, exclusion of red events from the track record, scope containment, demote-on-red, and verifier coverage — **and** the Operator-set bar sits at or above those floors. Both a *missing* bar and an *explicit-but-weak* bar fail closed: that a human typed a number proves the value was touched, not that it is safe, so the floor — never the act of setting a bar — is the load-bearing control. A pre-approved auto-promotion is further bounded: it applies only to a verifier-gated lane (per the verifier-gate boundary below) and only to risk levels already eligible for an autonomous proceed — never to orange, red, compliance-forced, or any always-escalate class — and every promotion is recorded and remains reversible fleet-wide. Production release is never pre-approved this way; it is always a per-event Operator approval. The **first** autonomous merge a deployment ever performs is not left unwitnessed and does *not* mint a third gate: a deployment's pre-approved auto-merge lanes may activate only after its first production-release decision, which explicitly records whether they may fire — folding the deployment's debut-autonomy beat into the existing production-release gate. This requires that "deployment identity" and "first autonomous merge" be a precise, non-gameable mechanism derivable from system state alone, with an unknown risk class failing closed. *(Earned trust, the risk-class merge ramp, and per-deployment autonomy — including pre-approved earn-in auto-promotion and its safety floors — are owned and enforced by FUNC-AC-FLEET; the merge decision and the earn-in evaluation are owned by FUNC-AC-MERGE-DECISION.)*
- Never acts on self-generated proposals without the Operator's approval — all work begins on the human's word.
- Never proceeds past a deployment's cost circuit breaker or budget ceiling without escalating to the Operator; spend is bounded per deployment, and an approaching limit raises a decision rather than silently overspending or stalling. *(The cost circuit breaker, workspace containment, and credential isolation are owned and enforced by FUNC-AC-SAFETY.)*
- Never autonomously *finalizes* specification content — the Operator approves it — and never authors or edits the vision (L0). The platform may draft, and the product owner may *suggest*, lower-layer specs under guardrails; deciding their content is always the Operator's.
- Never modifies its own implementation or its own evaluation and approval criteria.
- The coordinator never merges, never deploys, never alters a pipeline phase, and never edits the vision.
- All permanent knowledge changes — promoting a pattern, adapting a learned behavior, changing shared guidance — happen within these guardrails; sensitive knowledge is never auto-promoted, and a bad learned bias or a bad shared change must be reversible across the fleet.
- An honest boundary of what cannot be automated is a **per-deployment property, not the platform's**: each deployment carries its own map of what is automatable (green), what strains (yellow), and what is irreducibly human (red). The platform optimizes for concentrating the Operator's scarce attention on each deployment's irreducibly-human work — it does not pretend that work away.
- The scope verification of changes against their declared lane, and the escalate-only nature of risk markings, are never configurable — no deployment profile, lane declaration, or learned behavior can weaken them. *(Owned by FUNC-AC-MERGE-DECISION.)*
- **Verifier-gated autonomy.** A lane may earn autonomous *execution* only if it declares a verifier — an oracle that can return a real *failing* verdict on the domain **outcome** of incorrect work (tests, CI, a deployable diff, or any deterministic or independent check). The bar is the outcome, not a shell around it: a check that only confirms format, banned-claims, checklist completeness, arithmetic, or approval metadata is not a verifier, because it cannot fail on work that is wrong where it matters. A bare model-as-judge does **not** qualify as a verifier. A lane with no qualifying verifier is **assist-and-escalate only**: it may draft, surface decisions, and act on the Operator's approval, but never executes autonomously. No-oracle domains are therefore assist-and-escalate *by default*, with a genuine **earn-path**: a lane reaches autonomy only by engineering a deterministic or synthetic oracle that demonstrably rejects a real, judgment-level failure on the outcome — proven, never assumed, before the lane earns autonomous action. This generalizes the platform's "no verification, no merge" rule to all work — *no verifier, no autonomous action*. Like the scope tripwire, it is never configurable: no deployment profile, lane declaration, or learned behavior can grant a no-verifier lane autonomous execution, nor can it downgrade what counts as a qualifying verifier. *(Owned and enforced by FUNC-AC-VERIFIER-GATE.)*
- Generalizing for hypothetical other users at the expense of the Operator's fit is a vision violation, not a roadmap item.

**Success:** The Operator steers from one calm surface and is reached only by decisions that genuinely need them — a risky change, a specification suggestion, an ambiguous finding. Predictable pipeline work lands autonomously within each deployment's earned risk class; the system measurably asks the Operator less over time for decision-classes it has learned, while never crossing the boundaries above. A recurring mistake gets structurally prevented from recurring. Adding a project is a deployment-registry entry, not a fork. Every superseded plan is explicitly marked; the canonical specs have no live contradictor. A new kind of autonomous system is assembled from declared parts on the existing engine — without changing the engine. Steering the fleet requires exactly one surface; a week of normal operation needs no other steering tool. A non-software domain is steered from the same one pane as code, having earned its place under the narrow-first expansion protocol rather than a roadmap; and no lane — in any domain — acts autonomously without a verifier that can return a real failing verdict on its outcome. And the original promise still holds: the Operator can set direction before bed and wake to working, reviewed, honestly-gated work — with confidence that cost, safety, quality, and compliance were maintained while the human's attention was spent elsewhere.
